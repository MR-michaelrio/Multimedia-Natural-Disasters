TSUNAMI

Tsunamis are giant waves that are brought on by earthquakes or underwater volcanic eruptions. The height of tsunami waves does not substantially rise as they approach the. ocean's depths. However, as the waves move inland, the ocean's depth declines, causing them to rise to higher heights. Rather than the distance from the wave's source, the speed of tsunami waves is determined by the depth of the ocean. Over deep waters, tsunami waves may move as quickly as jet planes, only slowing down when they encounter shallow waters.

Impact to Communities

In addition to claiming human lives, tsunamis also drastically reduce the number of insects, animals, plants, and natural resources.

The terrain is altered by a tsunami. It disrupts wildlife habitats, including bird nesting grounds, by uprooting trees and plants.

If harmful chemicals are washed into the water and contaminate marine life, land creatures drown and aquatic species die from pollution.

The effects of a tsunami on the environment include both natural and man-made elements, in addition to the landscape and animal life.

The most significant environmental issue that a tsunami-affected country faces is solid waste and disaster debris.
